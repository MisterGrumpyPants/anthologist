{% if category == 'contexts' %}

Historical terms referring to chronological periods, cultural epochs, artistic movements, political dynasties, &c.

Because all these terms serve in the same way to describe a selection's context — context that other selections might share — The Anthologist lumps these together into one category instead of struggling to draw and enforce fine lines. (e.g. Is the Renaissance a period, movement, epoch, or something else?)

{% elif category == 'forms' %}

Broad categories for the format of a selection's source material; the most general designations concerning content (such as "nonfiction", "fiction", and "poetry") and length ("short-form" or "long-form").

A word about "short-form" vs "long-form": A "short-form" source is a self-contained work readable in one or two temperate sittings by an average literate human (e.g. short stories, letters, articles, most essays). Though "short-form" sources may be heaped together and bound in the most massive of massive volumes, each could still be extracted and stand alone. (An encyclopedia, for instance, is long, but all of its content is short-form writing.) A "long-form" source was intended by its author to consume a more significant segment of the reader's lifespan over numerous sittings (e.g. novels and nonfiction books).

{% elif category == 'topics' %}

In nonfiction, "topics" are usually apparent, articulated, explicitly addressed. (Not always.) In most fiction, on the other hand, there always multiple layers to what the work is "about". The Anthologist designates both surface-level topics and more subtle themes in order to weave selections together in many and meaningful ways. (e.g. The Road Runner cartoons are about a predator pursuing prey; they are also about the ruthlessness of desert landscapes, Fate frustrating our best-laid plans, and tireless defiance of ultimate futility.)

{% elif category == 'styles' %}

Formal elements of the text that (a) are noteworthy, and (b) might help readers find more of what they like.

For example, if you're like me, you like Robert Burton's distinctive sprawling sentences and overflowing lists and would like to read other authors who use similar patterns (similarly contradicting the standard style of contemporary writing).
